\subsection*{Comment F}\label{comment_R1_F}

\begin{commentbox}
    Page 14, line 417, the authors write that the BRT algorithm gives "higher weights to observations with larger prediction errors". I would have thought the opposite?
\end{commentbox}

\begin{response}

Iteratively, the boosted regression tree algorithm fits models on the residuals of the previous model. If the model leads to low prediction error for an observation, the resulting residual for the next iteration is low. If instead the model leads to large prediction error, the resulting residual for the next iteration is large. That is, in each iteration, observations with larger prediction errors are more influential than observations with smaller prediction errors. We acknowledge that the current formulation is ambiguous. In response, we have changed the statement as follows:
\end{response}

\begin{revisionbox}
    As an extension to regression trees, the BRT algorithm (\texttt{XGBoost} by \textcite{Chen.2016}) fits a sequence of many individual regression trees, iteratively focusing more strongly on observations with larger prediction errors in previous iterations.
\end{revisionbox}

\begin{response}
    Thank you for your collection of comments, which we address by providing more details in the manuscript as well as here in response. In general, we estimate the household-level carbon intensity of consumption as country-sector-level carbon intensities of final aggregate household demand weighted by household-level consumption shares for different goods and services. Country-sector-level carbon intensities of final household demand comprise both direct carbon emissions of households (e.g., in the transport sector) as well as "indirect" emissions, i.e., industry emissions that can be attributed to household final demand.

\subsubsection*{Comment 1a}\label{comment_R2_1a}

\begin{commentbox}
    How are emissions from household transportation calculated? I ask about this sector specifically because it seems to be a key driver of heterogeneity (e.g., Figures B.10.2 and B.10.3). Does the process incorporate annualized production emissions, or only fuel use from driving? The only hint that I could get for this question is by looking up the corresponding sector from GTAP, which I believe is: “Land transport and transport via pipelines (otp)” (Appendix, page 61). I wonder if this is more representative of emissions from the transportation of goods (not people).
\end{commentbox}

\begin{response}
    We include both annualized production emissions and fuel use from driving.

    Emissions from household transportation are included in the calculation of household carbon intensity of consumption in multiple ways: First, households report expenditures on transport fuels (such as diesel or gasoline), which we link to the carbon emission intensity of the GTAP sector \textit{petroleum and coke} (\textit{p_c}). This sector, specifically, captures households direct emissions. See Figure X. Second, households report expenditures on transport services (such as public transport, air travel, shipping), which we link to the carbon emission intensity of the respective GTAP sectors \textit{land transport and transport via pipelines} (\textit{otp}), \textit{air transport} (\textit{atp}) and \textit{water transport}, respectively. These sectors capture households' indirect emissions. Lastly, households report expenditures on motorized and non-motorized vehicles, which we link to the carbon emission intensity of \textit{manufacture of motorized vehicles} (\textit{mvh}) and \textit{manufacture of other transport equipment} (\textit{otn}), again capturing households' indirect emissions occurring in the manufacturing process.

    We have followed this classification consistently when matching diverse country-specific consumption items to uniform GTAP sectors. Expenditures on public transport fits best to \textit{otp}, which is consistent with substantially lower carbon intensities across countries in comparison to transport fuels (\textit{p_c}). Importantly, we match single transport fuels at the item-level, i.e., assuming that households would use electricity as a transport fuel, this would be represented in higher expenditures on electricity.
    
\end{response}

\subsubsection*{Comment 1b}\label{comment_R2_1b}

\begin{commentbox}
    Are motorcycles and cars assumed to have the same emissions factors?
\end{commentbox}

\begin{response}
    The indirect emissions intensity of motorcycles and cars are identical for each country, as we match both consumption items to the same GTAP sector \textit{mvh}. This captures emissions occurring in the production process of both motorcycles and cars.

    However, such emissions refer only to expenditures on \textit{buying} motorcycles or cars, but not on \textit{using} them. Heterogeneity between households using a motorcycle or car are reflected in household expenditures on transport fuels.
\end{response}

\subsubsection*{Comment 1c}\label{comment_R2_1c}

\begin{commentbox}
    Are all cars assumed to have the same emissions factors? This could be problematic for your analysis, in case richer households have more fuel-efficient vehicles, for example.
\end{commentbox}

\begin{response}
    This is an important aspect, but our analysis is in fact agnostic towards individual fuel efficiency of cars. Assume richer households had more fuel-efficient vehicles and they would drive the same distance as poorer households. Then richer households would report lower expenditures on transport fuels, which would in turn lead to a lower household carbon intensity of consumption. In other words: Our outcome variable of interest is an expression of household use of technology.

    In absence of information on transport technology (e.g., fuel efficiency of cars) and transport behaviour (e.g., distance driven, individual driving behaviour), our approach captures how much households spend on transport fuels in comparison to their total expenditures. This is a relevant approximation for the costs that a carbon price (or any other climate policy) would incur on households.

\end{response}

\subsubsection*{Comment 1d}\label{comment_R2_1d}

\begin{commentbox}
    How about the emissions factors assumed for public transport?
\end{commentbox}

\begin{response}
    Expenditures on public transport fits best to \textit{land transport and transport via pipelines} (\textit{otp}), which is consistent with substantially lower carbon intensities across countries in comparison to transport fuels (\textit{p_c}).

    It is noteworthy that our calculation of country-sector-level carbon intensities of final demand makes use of information on household final demand from GTAP. That is, it accounts for the fact that household final demand for services from this sector refer primarily to {land transport} and not to \textit{transport via pipelines}.
    
\end{response}

\subsubsection*{Comment 1e}\label{comment_R2_1e}

\begin{commentbox}
    Overall, simplifying assumptions related to the points above might mask substantial heterogeneity across households, so I wonder if this process should be refined with complementary data sources.
\end{commentbox}

\begin{response}
    We are interested in understanding heterogeneity among households in costs incurred by climate policy such as carbon pricing. The carbon intensity of consumption expresses how many kgCO\textsubscript{2} can on average be attributed to one unit of consumption (see Appendix X). Our measure reflects whether a household spends money on private transportation (through tracking expenditures on vehicles and carbon-intensive transport fuels) or on public transportation (through tracking expenditures on public transportation), but also how much a household spends on transportation in comparison to all other goods and services. Households which spend relatively more money on transport fuels are more likely to expect higher additional costs in the case of carbon pricing. This holds irrespectively of \textit{why} this household spends relatively more money on transport fuels, e.g., because of fuel-inefficient vehicles or because of greater demand for private transportation.

    What is correct is that we attribute carbon emissions and carbon emission intensities based on observed expenditures, but not based on (unobserved) carbon emissions occurring in production. Assume two car producers A and B with the same production process, but B offers their product with a 100\% price increase compared to A. As we -- by definition -- assume homogeneous goods within sectors, this implies that we cannot observe whether a household has bought two vehicles from producer A or one vehicle from producer B. This is a common shortcoming of microsimulations using input-output data. Addressing this problem would require plant-level information about production processes and highly disaggregated household consumption data, which we have not come across so far. Nevertheless, our approach captures differences in production processes between countries, between sectors and differences in household demand for goods and services from different countries and sectors. GTAP includes data for 161 countries and 65 sectors, which strikes a great balance in comparison to other input-output datasets.
    
\end{response}
